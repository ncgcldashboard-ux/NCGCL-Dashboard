Pakistan Yield Curve Analysis

March 2–30, 2026: Market Expectations & Interest Rate Dynamics

What Happened: In March 2026, Pakistan's yield curve shifted sharply upward as market participants repriced expectations for future interest rates. Despite the State Bank of Pakistan holding its policy rate steady, yields across the curve increased by 16 basis points (1-week) to 198 basis points (3-year). This shift reflects market expectations of sustained higher rates driven by fiscal pressures from government fuel subsidies and the escalating Iran geopolitical crisis. The peak shift occurred at the 2–3 year maturity, signaling that markets expect interest rates to remain elevated over the near-to-medium term.

Why Did the Yield Curve Shift?

1. The Geopolitical Shock: Iran Crisis Escalation

In early March 2026, escalating tensions in Iran created global uncertainty about energy markets and regional stability. While Pakistan is not directly engaged, the geopolitical risk premium increased risk appetite requirements across emerging markets. Despite the State Bank of Pakistan maintaining its policy rate, market participants immediately began repricing yields upward as a risk buffer.

2. Market Expectations: "Rates Will Stay Higher for Longer"

The yield curve shift is not driven by what the State Bank is doing today, but by what the market expects the State Bank will need to do in the future. Market participants began pricing in expectations of persistently higher interest rates through 2027 and beyond. This is evident from the 198 bps increase at the 3-year mark—the market is saying: "We expect rates to be significantly higher two to three years from now."

3. Fiscal Deficit Expectations: Fuel Subsidies & Government Borrowing

The Mechanism: The Government of Pakistan continues heavy fuel subsidies to keep petrol and diesel prices below market levels. These subsidies create a fiscal deficit—the government must spend more money than it collects in taxes. To finance this deficit, the government must borrow more money. When the government borrows more, it increases the demand for capital. With demand for funds exceeding supply, the cost of borrowing (interest rates) must rise.

The Pricing: Market participants expect this dynamic to persist and worsen. The peak spread at the 2–3 year mark reflects the consensus view that government borrowing needs will remain elevated for at least two years, keeping rates high.

4. Why the 2–3 Year Mark Saw the Biggest Shift

The 198 basis point increase at the 3-year maturity reveals market thinking:

  • Near-term (1 week to 3 months): Smaller increases (16–93 bps) reflect modest expectations of immediate SBP action.
  • 2–3 year horizon: Maximum increases (192–198 bps) reflect peak expectations that rates will rise significantly as fiscal pressures mount.
  • Long-term (5–10 years): Moderating increases (145–118 bps) suggest belief that fiscal pressures will eventually be addressed, allowing gradual rate normalization.
The inverted U-shape of the spread curve tells a story: markets expect high rates for 2–3 years, then gradual improvement.

Market Implications for Investors & Borrowers

For Bond Investors

Negative for Existing Holdings: If you owned 2–3 year bonds on March 2, the 198 bps jump in yields means your bond's market value has fallen significantly. A bond with 4–5 year duration loses approximately 8% in value for a 200 bps yield increase.

Opportunity Going Forward: The higher yields now offer better entry points for new purchases. Investors can lock in 12.5%+ yields on 3-year bonds, compensating for the expectation of rates staying high.

For Borrowers (Government & Corporates)

Higher Funding Costs: The Government of Pakistan's latest bond auctions are at 12.47% (2-year) and 12.48% (3-year)— up significantly from 10.55% and 10.50% just 28 days earlier. This increases the burden of servicing the debt.

Cascading Effects: Corporate borrowers face even higher spreads. If a corporate must pay 300 bps over government bonds, their 3-year cost is now 15.48%—making capital investment decisions more challenging.

What This Tells Us About Future Rates

The yield curve embedded in March 30 data is a market forecast of future short rates. The forward rate from year 2 to year 3 is approximately 12.49%, suggesting the market expects 1-year rates to be near 12.5% in March 2027 and March 2028. The State Bank's current policy rate doesn't matter—what matters is where the market expects it to be, and that's significantly higher than today.

Key Takeaways

The Policy Rate Didn't Change, But Market Rates Did

The State Bank's policy rate is set by monetary policy decisions. But market interest rates are set by expectations and supply-demand. When geopolitical risk rises or fiscal pressures mount, market rates adjust immediately—even without policy changes.

The Yield Curve Is a Forecast

The upward-sloping curve with peak at 2–3 years is the market's way of saying: "Rates will be elevated for the next 2–3 years, then gradually normalize." This forecast is embedded in every bond price.

Fiscal Policy Drives Long-Term Rates

While the State Bank controls the short-term policy rate, long-term rates are driven by expectations about fiscal deficits, government borrowing, and inflation. Pakistan's fuel subsidies directly impact the 5–10 year yield curve through this mechanism.

Duration Risk Was Real

Investors who held long-duration bonds (high interest rate sensitivity) suffered losses when the curve shifted. A 4.5-year duration bond loses roughly 9% in value for a 200 bps yield increase. This is why duration management is critical.
