The "Kill Switch" for Captive Power

The strongest strategic argument for TPT is that it makes Grid Power cheaper than Captive Gas generation. This reverses the industrial exodus.

Key Insight: TPT Marginal Cost (16.1 PKR) is ~50% cheaper than Captive Gas. Once fixed costs (CSC) are paid, every extra unit is far cheaper on the Grid.

The "Solar Sponge" Risk

With flat export rates, prosumers sell expensive solar when the system doesn't need it (mid-day) and draw power when the system is stressed (evening).

Strategic Correction

  • Depress Mid-Day Export Rates

    Drop export tariff to ~9 PKR/kWh during "Solar Sponge" (10am-3pm). Discourages dumping on the grid when system marginal price is low.

  • Incentivize Storage

    Increase export tariff to ~20 PKR/kWh during Peak (6pm-10pm). Prosumers install batteries to store noon sun and sell at evening peak.

  • Fair Capacity Recovery

    Net Metering users must pay CSC on their Gross Import Capacity, not Net Units. They use the grid as a backup battery; they must pay rent for the "wire."

Implementation Roadmap: Phase 1 to Maturity

Phase 1: Voluntary Opt-In

Immediate

Launch TPT for Industrial, Commercial, and High-Use Domestic (>700 units) on a voluntary basis.

  • Use Actual MDI where AMI exists.
  • Use Inferred MDI (kWh / LF Hours) as fallback, with safeguards.
  • Monthly national true-up for revenue neutrality.

Phase 2: Data Strengthening (60-90 Days)

Short Term

Fix the data gaps identified in the Policy Note to transition from "Inferred" to "Actual".

  • MDI Capture normalization across all DISCOs.
  • Sanctioned Load vs. Actual MDI audit.
  • Introduce "Coincident Peak" monitoring for advanced pricing.

Phase 3: Advanced ToE & Transition

Mid Term

Full integration of Prosumers and Global Best Practices (Australia/California Model).

  • Mandate Time-of-Export (ToE) pricing to manage "Solar Sponge".
  • Move from Opt-In to Opt-Out for Industrial consumers.
  • Dynamic Seasonal adjustments to CSC.

Global Precedent: California (NEM 3.0)

California faced the same "Duck Curve" issue. They moved to NEM 3.0, which drastically reduced export rates during solar hours and introduced electrification rates (low energy charge, high fixed charge) to encourage EV and Heat Pump adoption.

Australia (Victoria)

Implemented the "Solar Sponge" tariff. Network charges are high during evening peaks but drop significantly during the day to encourage consumers to run washing machines and pool pumps when solar is abundant.
