Executive Summary
A Structured, Forward-Looking Credit Risk Framework for NCGCL's Guarantee Portfolio

NCGCL issues partial credit guarantees to participating financial institutions (PFIs) against loans extended to SMEs, agriculture borrowers, exporters and other underserved segments. The institution's financial obligations are the claim payments it must make when underlying borrowers default. Measuring and managing those obligations with precision is not a regulatory formality; it is the operational core of a guarantee company. This policy note presents the architecture of NCGCL's integrated credit risk model, explains each component in detail, and seeks Board approval for the proposed macroeconomic scenarios that govern the forward-looking probability of default (PD) module.

The framework is built in three interlocking layers. The first layer reconstructs historical default rates for every participating financial institution and every economic sector, using 2011 to 2025 annual FI data and 39 quarters of SBP EasyData from Q3 2016 to Q1 2026. The second layer blends these historical anchors with a current FI risk scorecard to produce a composite PD for each guarantee facility. The third layer applies a macro-logit model to translate scenario-specific GDP, industrial output and interest rate paths into conditional forward-looking PD curves across three forecast years. Together these produce the probability-weighted 12-month and lifetime PDs required by IFRS 9.

The selected model is a credibility-shrunk anchored macro-logit. It does not override the historical anchor; it adjusts the historical anchor in log-odds space in response to the macroeconomic scenario. Scenario weights of 20 percent Upside, 55 percent Base and 25 percent Downside are proposed by management and are subject to Board approval. Until Board approval is granted, production ECL calculations remain disabled. The model in its current form is a benchmark calibration, not a production financial-reporting estimate.

Section 1
NCGCL and the Financial Guarantee Business

NCGCL is Pakistan's only Shariah-compliant credit guarantee institution, rated AAA/A1+ by PACRA. Its mandate is to catalyze lending to productive but underserved segments by bearing a portion of the credit risk that commercial banks are unwilling to hold at scale. It does not lend directly. It guarantees: when a bank lends to an SME or an agriculture borrower under an NCGCL-covered programme and that borrower defaults, NCGCL pays the bank the contractual claim up to the guaranteed amount. The institution then pursues recovery from the defaulting borrower and the PFI's contractual contribution.

This business model creates a specific risk architecture. NCGCL's loss depends on three quantities measured at the borrower level rather than at the bank level: (a) the probability that the underlying borrower defaults, (b) the loss given that default occurs net of recoveries, and (c) the exposure at the time of default. The bank's own creditworthiness affects NCGCL's risk only to the extent that it influences recovery on PFI contractual obligations after a claim is settled; the primary credit event is the underlying borrower default.

Historically, NCGCL used fixed PD multipliers and sector-level NPL rates embedded in a management dashboard to estimate expected loss and price guarantees. That approach, while operationally simple, does not satisfy IFRS 9's requirement for probability-weighted forward-looking ECL or the SICR (significant increase in credit risk) assessment needed to determine whether a facility should be staged from 12-month to lifetime ECL. The model presented in this note addresses both requirements without discarding the management risk framework that already governs pricing and capital allocation.

Section 2
The FI Risk Scoring Framework

Before a guarantee is issued, NCGCL assesses the creditworthiness of the PFI, not because the PFI is the primary obligor, but because PFI quality affects three things that matter to NCGCL: the PFI's portfolio underwriting standards (which determine what borrowers end up in the guaranteed pool), the PFI's ability to pursue recoveries on NCGCL's behalf after a claim, and the PFI's contractual commitment to retain a skin-in-the-game portion of every loss. A weak PFI poses moral hazard; a strong PFI is a disciplined origination partner.

The FI scorecard operates on a five-point scale across two dimensions: quantitative and qualitative. Quantitative factors are scored automatically from the embedded FI historical database and the current regulatory filing. Qualitative factors are scored by the relationship team and reviewed by the credit committee. The combined score maps to a current FI rating modifier that adjusts the sector-derived PD upward or downward in log-odds space before the composite PD is finalized.

Section 3
Institutional Probability of Default Estimation

NCGCL maintains a historical PD register for every bank, DFI and microfinance bank active in Pakistan from 2011 to 2025. These are not the counterparty PDs of the FIs themselves (which would require a separate credit analysis of the FI's own bonds or deposits). They are the PDs of the lending books managed by each FI, reconstructed from publicly reported NPL and advances data. An FI that consistently generates higher NPLs than its peers is signaling poor underwriting; its borrower pool in a new guaranteed portfolio is likely to exhibit a higher default frequency.

The reconstruction uses a four-step process. First, gross advances and NPL stocks are sourced from SBP EasyData and FI financial statements for each year. Second, the annualised positive net-stock flow proxy is calculated: the increase in NPL stock in a given year, divided by the opening performing book, and annualised. Third, the mean of this series across the available history gives the FI's long-run historical PD proxy. Fourth, this is credibility-weighted against the peer median to produce the FI historical performance effect.

Why a Flow Proxy, Not a Stock Ratio?

NPL stock ratios (infection ratios) are not default rates. They accumulate historical defaults, recoveries, write-offs and reclassifications. A high infection ratio may reflect poor past recoveries, not high current default rates. The positive net-stock flow proxy removes the stock accumulation and focuses on the year-on-year increase: if NPLs rise, new defaults are outpacing cures and recoveries. This is a closer, though imperfect, approximation of the annual default rate than the stock ratio. The limitation is acknowledged: gross new default entries are not available from public sources, and the proxy likely undercounts defaults in years of heavy write-off activity. An explicit margin of conservatism is applied to compensate.

FI Historical PD Proxy and Credibility Adjustment
// Step 1: Annual positive net NPL flow proxy FlowPD_t = max(0, (NPL_t - NPL_{t-1})) / PerformingAdvances_{t-1} // Step 2: Long-run FI PD proxy (mean over available years) HistPD_i = mean(FlowPD_t) for t in {2012...2023} // Step 3: Peer PD (median positive TTC within subcategory or category) PeerPD_p = median({HistPD_j : j in PeerGroup_p, HistPD_j > 0}) // Step 4: Raw odds ratio RawOddsRatio_i = (HistPD_i / (1 - HistPD_i)) / (PeerPD_p / (1 - PeerPD_p)) // Step 5: Credibility-weighted FI effect (in log-odds) FIEffect_i = Credibility_i * ln(RawOddsRatio_i) // Step 6: Adjusted odds multiplier AdjustedMultiplier_i = exp(FIEffect_i) // Credibility function (Buhlmann-Straub approximation): Credibility_i = YearsActual_i / (YearsActual_i + kappa) // where kappa is calibrated to produce approximately 50% credibility at 5 years // Effect is floored at 0.5x and capped at 2.0x odds ratio

A credibility factor of zero means the FI's own history is ignored and only the peer median applies; a factor of one means the FI's own history is applied in full. In the current calibration, FIs with 13 years of history receive credibility factors ranging from 0.19 to 0.75 depending on the variance and consistency of their historical series. FIs with nil-NPL records receive credibility factors but carry a zero raw odds ratio, so they revert to the peer benchmark without a discount.

Section 4
Sectoral Probability of Default Estimation

The underlying borrower PD is not derived from the FI's book-level NPL alone; it requires a disaggregation to the sector or segment level that the guaranteed portfolio actually covers. NCGCL does not guarantee entire bank books; it guarantees specific pools of SME loans, agriculture credit or export financing. The relevant default rate is therefore the historical rate in those segments, not the blended bank-level rate. Sectoral PDs are reconstructed from SBP's quarterly NPL and advances data, disaggregated by sector and segment, extending from Q3 2016 to Q1 2026.

The flow method is applied at the segment level: for each quarter, the positive net change in NPL stock is divided by the opening performing balance to approximate the quarterly new-default rate. This is annualised by multiplying by four (reflecting quarterly frequency). The mean of this series over the available window forms the historical anchor PD for that sector or segment. The word "anchor" is deliberate: the anchor is not a forecast; it is the long-run central tendency around which macroeconomic adjustments are applied.

Limitation: Flow Proxy vs True Default Rate

The annualised flow proxy systematically undercounts true defaults in periods when write-offs are high (write-offs remove NPLs from the stock, suppressing the net change). It overcounts in periods when NPL reclassifications add back previously performing loans without new underlying defaults. These limitations are disclosed, and an explicit log-odds margin of conservatism (MOC) is added at the final PD computation stage to partially compensate for the likely undercount.

The sector or segment anchor is the single most important input to the model. It sets the level around which all macroeconomic adjustments are made. An error in the anchor propagates through every downstream calculation. NCGCL must therefore invest in obtaining granular NPL flow data (gross new defaults, cures, write-offs and reclassifications separately) from PFIs to eventually replace the positive net-stock proxy with a true annualised default rate. This is the highest-priority remediation action in the model limitation register.

Section 5
Composite (Blended) Probability of Default

A guarantee facility rarely covers a single sector. An SME portfolio may have borrowers in textile, food processing, auto parts and services, with weights that shift as the PFI originates new loans. NCGCL's facility register therefore captures a sector allocation vector for each guarantee: the percentage of the underlying financing exposed to each sector or segment. The sector prior PD is the weighted average of individual sector PDs using these allocation weights.

Section 6
Guarantee Structures and Risk Profiles

Not all guarantees carry the same risk even if the underlying borrower pool is identical. The risk that NCGCL actually bears depends on where in the loss distribution NCGCL's obligation sits. A guarantee that covers the very first losses in a pool is the most risky position because every loss, starting from the first rupee, falls on NCGCL. A guarantee that covers only losses above a high threshold is far less risky because losses must exceed a substantial cushion before NCGCL pays anything. NCGCL currently offers three structural variants: pari-passu, first-loss and second-loss (tranche) guarantees.

The expected loss on a first-loss guarantee is higher than on a pari-passu or second-loss guarantee at the same underlying PD because the first-loss layer is the most likely to be triggered. The economic capital required for a first-loss guarantee is also higher per rupee of limit, because the unexpected loss (the tail above the expected loss) that NCGCL must absorb is more concentrated.

First-Loss Concentration Risk: A first-loss guarantee provides the PFI with the maximum credit risk transfer, making it highly attractive to originate new lending. But it concentrates NCGCL's exposure in the highest-probability loss zone. NCGCL must price first-loss guarantees to reflect the full expected loss of the tranche, not the expected loss of the pool. At a 5.15% Agriculture anchor PD and 50% LGD, the expected annual loss rate on a first-loss layer covering the first 10% of an agriculture portfolio is approximately 5.15% * 50% = 2.58% of the pool, or 25.8% of the first-loss layer limit. This requires commensurately higher capital allocation and guarantee fees.
Section 7
Expected Credit Loss Calculation

IFRS 9 requires that the loss allowance for a financial guarantee contract reflect the expected cash shortfalls discounted to the reporting date. For NCGCL, the cash shortfall is the claim payment it expects to make, net of recoveries it expects to receive. This is measured differently depending on whether the guarantee has experienced a significant increase in credit risk (SICR) since initial recognition. If it has not, a 12-month expected loss is recognised (Stage 1). If it has, the lifetime expected loss is recognised (Stage 2). If the guaranteed pool is already credit-impaired, a credit-impaired ECL is recognised (Stage 3).

Exhibit 7-1
IFRS 9 Three-Stage Classification and ECL Measurement
Significant Increase in Credit Risk (SICR)

SICR is determined by comparing the current lifetime PD to the original lifetime PD at the date of initial recognition. The quantitative SICR test fires if the relative increase in lifetime PD exceeds 3.0 times (i.e. lifetime PD has more than tripled) or the absolute increase exceeds 2.0 percentage points. A qualitative SICR backstop applies for any facility that is 30 days past due, on the watchlist, restructured or has received a credit-quality downgrade. Where original recognition PD is unavailable, Stage 2 is applied conservatively as a fallback.

SICR Quantitative Test
// Relative increase test RelativeIncrease = CurrentLifetimePD / OriginalLifetimePD // Absolute increase test AbsoluteIncrease = CurrentLifetimePD - OriginalLifetimePD // SICR fires if EITHER condition is met: QuantitativeSICR = (RelativeIncrease > 3.0) OR (AbsoluteIncrease > 0.02) // Final Stage assignment: Stage = 3 if DefaultFlag = True or DaysPastDue >= 90 Stage = 2 if QuantitativeSICR OR QualitativeSICR Stage = 1 otherwise (or if LowCreditRisk simplification applies)
ECL Formula and Discount Rate

The IFRS 9 ECL for a non-defaulted guarantee is the sum across forecast years of the marginal unconditional PD multiplied by the LGD, the exposure at default, the guarantee allocation factor and the discount factor. The marginal PD in each year is the probability of defaulting in that specific year given survival to the start of that year. Discounting brings the expected future payments back to the reporting date using a rate appropriate for the time value of money on guarantee liabilities.

IFRS 9 ECL Calculation (Non-Defaulted Guarantee)
// For scenario s, summed across years y = 1 to T: ECL_s = Σ_y [ MarginalPD_s,y * EAD_s,y * LGD_s,y * GuaranteeAlloc_y * DF_y ] // Survival and marginal PD: Survival_y = Survival_{y-1} * (1 - ConditionalPD_s,y) ; Survival_0 = 1 MarginalPD_y = Survival_{y-1} * ConditionalPD_s,y CumulativePD_y = 1 - Survival_y // Discount factor (annual compounding): DF_y = 1 / (1 + r)^y where r = contractual or risk-free rate // Probability-weighted ECL: PWECL = Σ_s weight_s * ECL_s // Stage 1 selects 12-month ECL (y = 1 only) // Stage 2 selects lifetime ECL (y = 1 to T) // Stage 3 uses credit-impaired formula (gross claim less PV of recoveries)
Subsequent Measurement

IFRS 9 paragraph 4.2.1(d) requires that the carrying amount of a financial guarantee be measured at the higher of (a) the loss allowance (ECL) and (b) the initial recognition amount less cumulative income recognised. This ensures that if ECL is lower than the unamortised initial recognition amount, the initial recognition amount governs. As a guarantee approaches maturity and cumulative income recognised increases, the unamortised initial amount falls and ECL typically governs. Any change in the recognised liability from one period to the next flows through the income statement.

Section 8
Economic Capital and Capital Adequacy

Expected loss is what NCGCL expects to pay on average over the life of a guarantee; it is addressed through IFRS 9 provisioning. Economic capital is what NCGCL needs to hold as an equity buffer to absorb unexpected losses, those that exceed the expected level with low but non-trivial probability. The distinction matters: provisioning replaces the expected loss in the income statement as it arises; capital is a balance sheet buffer that exists precisely because losses can be worse than expected.

NCGCL's economic capital model applies a one-factor loss distribution approach. For each guarantee tranche, the unexpected loss is the standard deviation of the loss distribution at the selected confidence level. For a portfolio guarantee with PD, LGD and asset correlation, the IRB formula provides a theoretical benchmark. In practice, NCGCL applies a conservative floor and supervisory fallback to avoid understating capital when data are sparse. The economic capital rate is the higher of three quantities: the exact tranche unexpected loss, an attachment-and-detachment sensitive supervisory fallback based on a benchmark pool capital rate, and a 2 percent absolute floor.

Economic Capital Rate and Capital Allocation
// IRB-style pool unexpected loss benchmark (for reference): K_pool = LGD * [ N( G(PD)/sqrt(1-R) + sqrt(R/(1-R)) * G(0.999) ) - PD ] // N() = standard normal CDF, G() = standard normal quantile, R = asset correlation // Tranche unexpected loss (via simulation or one-factor approximation): UL_tranche = f(K_pool, A, D, PD, LGD) // attachment A, detachment D // Supervisory fallback: K_fallback = K_pool * LayerSensitivityFactor(A, D) // Final economic capital rate: EC_Rate = max(UL_tranche, K_fallback, 0.02) // Internal risk weight (calibrated against 20% reference CAR): InternalRW = EC_Rate / 0.20 // Capital allocated: AllocatedCapital = InternalRW * SelectedCAR * GuaranteeLimit // RAROC (pre-tax): RAROC = (FeeIncome - ExpectedLoss - OperatingCost) / AllocatedCapital

The internal risk weight framework ensures that capital allocation is independent of the selected CAR. Changing the CAR from 20 percent to 15 percent does not change the underlying economic capital rate or internal risk weight; it changes only the rupee amount of capital allocated per unit of guarantee limit. This preserves the integrity of RAROC calculations, which must reflect the true economic risk of each guarantee, not an accounting choice about how much capital to hold against it.

Capital Adequacy Ratio vs Economic Capital Rate

The capital adequacy ratio (CAR) is an accounting or regulatory concept that measures the ratio of regulatory capital to risk-weighted assets. Economic capital is a risk management concept that measures the capital needed to absorb unexpected losses at a given confidence level. For a financial guarantee company with no deposit liabilities and no formal banking licence, regulatory CAR in the traditional sense does not apply. NCGCL uses an internally-defined CAR to express capital allocation in a form comparable to banking-sector metrics and to communicate risk appetite to the Board in a familiar framework. The internal risk weight is the bridge between the two concepts.

Practical Illustration: A first-loss SME guarantee with a 15% economic capital rate and a PKR 100M limit requires allocated capital of (15% / 20%) * 20% * 100M = PKR 15M. At a 100 bps guarantee fee, annual fee income is PKR 1M. Annual expected loss is approximately PKR 0.99M (2.19% * 45% * 100M). RAROC = (1.0 - 0.99) / 15.0 = 0.7%. This is below the 15% hurdle and the guarantee requires re-pricing to be value-accretive. The model makes this visible before commitment.
Section 9
Notional Leverage versus Risk Capital

A common but misleading metric for guarantee companies is the leverage ratio: total guarantees outstanding divided by equity. This metric treats every rupee of guarantee as equally risky, which it is not. A PKR 500M pari-passu SME guarantee with 20% cover and a 2% underlying PD is fundamentally different from a PKR 500M first-loss agriculture guarantee with 100% cover and a 5% underlying PD. Notional leverage conflates these two, producing a number that looks alarming but carries no information about actual risk.

The appropriate frame for a guarantee company is risk-adjusted leverage: how much economic capital has been deployed relative to the capital base, and how does the expected loss under the stress scenario compare to equity? NCGCL's leverage headroom of up to 10 times equity is a notional maximum, not an approved operational target. The binding constraint is the economic capital utilisation ratio, measured as total allocated capital divided by total capital base. The Board has set an operating cap of 80 percent utilisation, which means no more than PKR 6,400M of capital (80% of PKR 8,000M) can be committed to guarantee exposure at any time.

Section 10
Forward-Looking Probability of Default Calibration

A historical anchor PD is a backward-looking quantity. IFRS 9 explicitly requires that ECL incorporate reasonable and supportable forward-looking information about future conditions. Using only the historical anchor would be correct only if the future is expected to be identical to the past. In Pakistan's macroeconomic context, with significant variation in GDP growth (ranging from negative in COVID years to 5-6 percent in boom periods), interest rate cycles of 800 to 1,000 basis points, and periodic exchange rate stress, the historical anchor underestimates ECL when conditions are deteriorating and overestimates it when conditions are improving. The forward-looking calibration corrects this by shifting the anchor PD up or down based on the expected path of key macroeconomic variables.

The selected methodology is a credibility-shrunk anchored macro-logit model. The term "anchored" reflects the design decision that the model is centered on the historical anchor, not on a regression intercept. This prevents the model from producing absurd PDs in periods of extreme macroeconomic stress when regression extrapolation would typically fail. The term "macro-logit" reflects that the macroeconomic adjustment is made in log-odds space, preserving the property that PD is always between zero and one. Credibility shrinkage keeps the macro coefficients conservative and the overall PD close to the historical anchor when data are sparse.

Term Structure Generation

The model generates annual conditional PDs for three explicit forecast years, plus mean reversion to the long-run anchor for years four through six, followed by the anchor level thereafter. The three-year forecast horizon aligns with the macroeconomic scenario paths approved by the Board. Mean reversion reflects the empirical observation that macroeconomic cycles are temporary; the long-run default rate tends to converge to the structural anchor as conditions normalise.

Floors, Caps and Margin of Conservatism

Three explicit guardrails are applied after the raw macro-adjusted PD is computed. The floor prevents the model from producing implausibly low PDs in optimistic scenarios: a sector with a 1.86% historical anchor PD should not produce a forward-looking PD of 0.05% just because macroeconomic conditions are favorable; some irreducible default rate always applies. The cap prevents implausible PDs in the extreme tail; while Pakistan's agriculture sector has experienced severe stress, a PD above 45% would be difficult to justify in a going-concern context. The margin of conservatism (MOC) is an additive log-odds uplift applied to every scenario to compensate for the likely undercount in the positive net-stock flow proxy and the imprecision inherent in a sparse-data estimation.

Section 11
Macroeconomic Factors and the Conditional PD Model

Pakistan's default cycles are driven by a recognisable pattern: industrial output contraction raises borrower stress and reduces debt service capacity, while high lending rates compound the burden for variable-rate borrowers. Exchange rate depreciation raises input costs for import-dependent sectors and export receipts for export-oriented ones, with asymmetric effects. The model captures these three channels using LSM growth, the outstanding lending rate and PKR depreciation as explanatory variables. All three are standardised (mean-zero, unit-variance) so that the coefficients express the sensitivity of log-odds PD to a one-standard-deviation shift in each variable from the historical mean.

Variable 1: LSM Growth (Large-Scale Manufacturing Index)

LSM growth is the most consistent predictor of borrower default frequency in NCGCL's calibration dataset. Negative LSM growth signals industrial contraction: factories run below capacity, revenues fall, payrolls are stressed and loan repayments become difficult. The coefficient is negative across all model groups, confirming that higher LSM growth reduces default probability. The Agriculture model has the largest sensitivity (beta = minus 0.707) because agricultural output directly determines the repayment capacity of the agriculture lending book. The SME model has a somewhat lower sensitivity (beta = minus 0.456) because SME cash flows are more diversified across sectors than pure agriculture.

Variable 2: Outstanding Lending Rate

The outstanding lending rate captures the debt service cost across the banking system's existing loan book. A higher rate raises the cost of servicing existing floating-rate loans and increases the hurdle for refinancing at maturity. The coefficient is positive across all model groups, confirming the economic intuition that higher borrowing costs raise default frequency. The SME and export-oriented models have the highest rate sensitivity (beta = 0.405) because SME and export borrowers tend to have shorter-term, variable-rate facilities. The agriculture model has a lower sensitivity (beta = 0.172) possibly because agriculture credit in Pakistan is partially subsidized and less responsive to market rate changes.

Variable 3: PKR Depreciation (Export-Oriented Only)

PKR depreciation is included only for export-oriented exposures because its effect is directionally ambiguous for general commercial borrowers: it raises input costs but also raises the PKR value of export revenues. For net exporters, depreciation improves revenue in PKR terms while costs are partially in PKR, improving repayment capacity. The coefficient for the export-oriented group is positive at 0.217, reflecting the observation that in Pakistan's recent depreciation episodes, input cost stress exceeded revenue benefits for the broader manufacturing sector. This coefficient is applied at a shrunk value to reflect uncertainty; it was set to zero for all other model groups where the evidence was unstable.

Annexure C
Statistical Model Specification, Validation and Sensitivity Evidence
C1. Model Selection and Candidate Comparison

Four candidate models were evaluated on the out-of-sample dataset (48 quarterly observations across core segments). The evaluation criteria were the Brier score (accuracy of probability predictions), the calibration ratio (ratio of predicted to realised PD, where 1.0 is ideal), economic sign consistency, scenario monotonicity and stability under sparse data. The credibility-shrunk anchored macro-logit was selected as the primary model based on governance considerations: it is the only candidate that produces a forward-looking empirical response to macroeconomic conditions while maintaining explicit calibration and stability controls. The anchor-only and fixed-multiplier challengers have better absolute Brier scores but provide no macroeconomic scenario sensitivity. The unconstrained group model has better in-sample fit but mixed signs and severe underprediction (calibration ratio 0.17x).

C2. Final Model Specification and Coefficients

The final model is estimated in log-odds space using pooled quarterly data from Q3 2016 to Q1 2026. The common model (SME + corporate + consumer pooled) provides the base LSM and lending-rate coefficients. Group-specific models scale these coefficients up or down using segment-specific data. The Agriculture model is re-estimated on agriculture segment data. The export-oriented model borrows the SME coefficients and adds a shrunk FX loading of 0.217 (the unstable FX coefficient from the corporate model, reduced by 50% as a shrinkage adjustment).

// KEY MATHEMATICAL FUNCTIONS USED IN THE MODEL // Logit function (maps probability to log-odds): logit(p) = ln( p / (1 - p) ) // Examples: logit(0.01) = -4.595; logit(0.05) = -2.944; logit(0.20) = -1.386 // Inverse logit / sigmoid (maps log-odds to probability): sigmoid(z) = exp(z) / (1 + exp(z)) = 1 / (1 + exp(-z)) // Examples: sigmoid(-4.595) = 0.010; sigmoid(0) = 0.500 // Credibility weighting (Buhlmann-Straub approximation): c = n / (n + kappa) // where n = years of observed data, kappa = credibility threshold // kappa ≈ 5 to 8 gives 50% credibility at 5-8 years of history // Log-odds addition (composing probability adjustments): p_combined = sigmoid( logit(p_base) + delta_1 + delta_2 + ... ) // This is the core of the PD blend operation: add in log-odds, invert. // Survival and marginal PD term structure: S_0 = 1.0 S_y = S_{y-1} * (1 - PD_y) // Survival to end of year y m_y = S_{y-1} * PD_y // Marginal (unconditional) PD for year y C_y = 1 - S_y // Cumulative PD through end of year y // Check: Sum of all m_y = 1 - S_T (probability of default by year T) // IRB-style pool unexpected loss (Vasicek one-factor model): K = LGD * [Phi( Phi_inv(PD)/sqrt(1-R) + sqrt(R/(1-R))*Phi_inv(0.999) ) - PD] // where Phi = standard normal CDF, Phi_inv = its quantile function // R = asset correlation (typically 0.03 to 0.20 for retail/SME) // 0.999 quantile = 99.9% confidence level // Discount factor (annual): DF_y = 1 / (1 + r)^y // where r = discount rate appropriate to the guarantee liability
